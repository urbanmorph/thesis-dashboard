üîç Hidden Dynamics: The Systemic Barriers

These systemic issues don't appear in official statistics but determine whether biodiversity interventions succeed or fail. Investment here creates leverage.

1. Ownership & Demands

  • Commons as "unowned": Forests seen as available land for development, not community resource [157]
  • Forest land = "development opportunity": Political pressure to divert for industry, infrastructure, mining
  • Indigenous rights denied: CFR claims systematically rejected‚Äîonly 6,300 titles vs 200,000+ claims [157]
  • Wetlands claimed by developers: "Wasteland" to be "reclaimed" and made "productive"
  • Wildlife corridors unrecognized: Not in land records‚Äîencroached without ecological assessment

2. Culture & Narrative

  • "Development vs environment" binary: Conservation framed as anti-progress, obstacle to jobs and growth
  • "Nature = outside cities": Biodiversity seen as forest/wildlife concern, not urban planning priority
  • Wildlife = threat: Human-animal conflict covered as "attack" stories, not habitat fragmentation consequences [167]
  • Tree planting as greenwashing: Numbers celebrated (X million planted!) without survival/ecological value tracking
  • Indigenous knowledge dismissed: Community conservation models invisible in policy discourse

3. Capacity & Skills

  • Ecological restoration expertise scarce: Forest departments lack ANR, soil bioengineering capacity
  • Urban forestry gaps: Municipal planners untrained in native species, ecological design [172]
  • Wetland management skills absent: City corporations don't know how to revive lakes, restore hydrology
  • Wildlife corridor design limited: Planners can't map animal movement, design crossings [159]
  • Natural capital accounting missing: Economists can't value biodiversity loss, integrate into cost-benefit [176]

4. Data & Evidence

  • Urban tree census missing: Most cities lack GIS inventory of species, age, canopy by neighborhood [171]
  • Green cover equity unmapped: No data on tree cover disparity between rich/poor areas
  • Biodiversity loss unquantified: Species extinction, population declines not systematically monitored
  • Wildlife corridor fragmentation unmapped: No GIS analysis of where roads/railways cut movement paths
  • CAMPA outcomes opaque: No public dashboard of projects, survival rates, ecological outcomes [165]

5. Policy & Regulation

  • Forest clearance rubber-stamped: FAC meetings approve 90%+ projects with weak scrutiny [179]
  • Wetland protection unenforced: Rules exist but state committees inactive, prosecutions rare
  • EIA diluted (2020): Post-facto clearances allowed, public hearing weakened, exemptions expanded [180]
  • Wildlife corridors legally unprotected: No enforceable restrictions on linear infrastructure
  • Biodiversity offsets absent: No requirement to restore 2x habitat when clearing forests

6. Finance & Capital

  • CAMPA funds underutilized: ‚Çπ66,000+ Cr accumulated but <40% spent due to rigid procedures [165]
  • Green bonds for biodiversity rare: Capital markets ignore forest restoration, wetland revival [175]
  • PES mechanisms absent: Communities protecting watersheds not compensated for services
  • Urban forestry budgets minuscule: 1-2% of municipal budgets; no maintenance funding [168]
  • Conservation ROI uncalculated: No business case showing water security, flood mitigation value

7. Market & Procurement

  • Native species nurseries limited: Most stock ornamental exotics; local ecotypes hard to procure at scale
  • Ecological restoration supply chains thin: Few firms with ANR, wetland revival expertise
  • Tender specs prioritize survival %: Contractors plant monocultures (eucalyptus, acacia) not biodiversity [165]
  • Community enterprises rare: CFR holders lack market linkages for NTFP, ecotourism, carbon credits
  • Green infrastructure procurement absent: No mandates for bioswales, green roofs in public works
