  • Policy asymmetry: RE gets viability gap funding + RPOs; efficiency gets voluntary programs

  • Grid planning blind: CEA projects capacity addition; never models demand reduction scenarios

  • Merit order ignored: Efficient plants underutilized while inefficient plants run for state revenue
  • Investment opportunity: Efficiency-based dispatch, accelerated retirement, waste heat recovery

üîç Hidden Dynamics Why decentralization hasn't worked

The 74th CAA's failure isn't accidental ‚Äî it serves powerful interests. Understanding these dynamics reveals why constitutional provisions remain on paper.

1. State Control Preservation

  • Deliberate election delay: States control delimitation to prevent SEC from holding elections; Telangana SEC had to go to High Court to compel state action [141]
  • Parastatal preference: Creating parallel bodies (UDAs, Water Corps) keeps power with state, not ULBs [141,142]
  • Overriding powers retained: States maintain power to approve ULB budgets, plans, bye-laws despite constitutional devolution [142]
  • Leverage: Autonomous State Election Commissions; transfer delimitation power per 2nd ARC recommendation

2. Fiscal Dependency Trap

  • Designed weakness: No constitutional provision for municipal revenue base ‚Äî states control all tax authority, rates, grants [141]
  • SFC delays: 6th SFC was due in 2019, 5th SFC recommendations still being implemented; delays of 49-903 days in constitution [142]
  • Unpredictable transfers: ULBs can't plan when grants are discretionary and delayed; ‚Çπ196 Cr shortfall in Odisha alone [142]
  • Leverage: Central Valuation Boards for property tax; predictable grant formulas; own revenue autonomy

3. Capacity Starvation

  • Controlled recruitment: State governments control staff assessment, recruitment, pay, transfers, disciplinary action ‚Äî not ULBs [142]
  • Declining strength: Sanctioned staff decreased despite population growth; 35-65% vacancies across cities [141,143]
  • Skill mismatch: First-time councillors unaware of municipal systems; commissioners lack urban governance experience [141]
  • Leverage: Municipal cadre establishment; mandatory induction training; stable commissioner tenures

4. Ward Committee Theater

  • Mandated but meaningless: Constitutional requirement exists but most states haven't notified implementation rules [143]
  • Political capture: Where formed, nominations are politically motivated; citizen participation absent [141]
  • No power: Advisory role only, no budget authority, no staff ‚Äî "bridge to nowhere" [142]
  • Leverage: Kerala/Gujarat models with functional ward committees; mandatory Area Sabha at sub-ward level

5. Councillor Devaluation

  • Pay disparity: Councillors earn ‚Çπ2,500-25,000/month vs MLAs ‚Çπ50,000-1,83,000 ‚Äî 10-50x gap [143]
  • Term mismatch: Mayor tenure 1-2.5 years in many states vs Panchayat presidents 5 years [143]
  • Indirect election: Most mayors indirectly elected; less accountable than directly elected counterparts [143]
  • Leverage: Five-year mayoral terms; direct mayoral election; councillor pay parity with MLAs

6. Small Town Neglect

  • Big city bias: JNNURM covered only 65 cities; small/medium towns can't access external funds [141]
  • Capacity cliff: Small towns can't prepare project reports to qualify for grants; 80% own revenue from Municipal Corporations only [141]
  • Population-blind staffing: Same population criterion regardless of area, complexity, growth rate [142]
  • Leverage: Differentiated support for small towns; regional capacity building centers; simplified funding access
